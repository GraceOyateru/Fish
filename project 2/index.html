What is a Fish?

Fish are aquatic vertebrate animals that have gills but lack limbs with digits, like fingers or toes. Recall that vertebrates are animals with internal backbones. Most fish are streamlined in their general body form

Types/Classes of Fishes

Fishes are typically divided into three groups;

  1. Superclass Agnatha (jawless fishes)
  2. Class Chondrichthyes (cartilaginous fishes)
  3. Superclass Osteichthyes (bony fishes)

Superclass Agnatha (jawless fishes)

Agnatha (no jaws) is a superclass of jawless fish in the phylum Chordata, subphylum Vertebrata, consisting of both present (cyclostomes) and extinct (conodonts and ostracoderms) species. The group is sister to all vertebrates with jaws, known as gnathostomes.

Class Chondrichthyes (cartilaginous fishes)

Chondrichthyes is a class that contains sharks, skates, rays and chimeras. They are jawed vertebrates, with skeletons made of cartilage instead of bone.

The class can be divided into two subclasses;

  • Elasmobranchii (sharks, rays, skates, and sawfish)
  • Holocephali (chimaeras).

Elasmobranchii(sharks,rays,skates,and sawfish)

Elasmobranchs have several rows of teeth which are continually replaced. They don't have swim bladders, but instead their large livers are full of oil to provide buoyancy.

Elasmobranchs reproduce sexually with internal fertilization and either bear live young or lay eggs.

Holocephali(chimaeras)

These are endoskeleton cartilaginous, often calcified. Persistent (unconstricted) notochord; poorly developed vertebrae. Holostylic jaw suspension, i.e., the upper jaw is immovably united with cranium, hence the name holocephali. Teeth united to form crushing plates, devoid of enamel.


Superclass Osteichthyes (bony fishes)

Osteichthyes, commonly referred to as the bony fish, is a diverse superclass of vertebrates that have skeletons primarily composed of bone tissue.

They can be contrasted with the Chondrichthyes, which have skeletons primarily composed of cartilage.

They can be further divided into two(2) subphylum Vertebrata classes;

  • Sarcopterygii (lobe-finned fishes)
  • Actinopterygii (ray-finned fishes)

Sarcopterygii (lobe-finned fishes)

These are fleshy pectoral and pelvic (paired) fins that articulate with the pectoral (shoulder) and pelvic (hip) girdles via a single bone

This is apparent in the coelacanths and lungfishes, which are more intuitively fish-like.

Actinopterygii (ray-finned fishes)

Actinopterygian fishes, also called ray-finned fishes, diverged from sarcopterygians, the lobe-fined fishes, some 450 millions years before present (MYBP).

They are found in every aquatic habitat from the abyssal depths of the ocean to freshwater streams and ponds; a few can even crawl on land for short periods of time. Ray-finned fishes constitute a major source of food for millions of people.
